What the ADL Is

How Donor Money Built the Architecture of Silence — And Why Afrikaners Are Outside It

For readers outside the United States

The Anti-Defamation League (ADL) is a private American non-profit organisation founded in 1913 in New York, originally to combat antisemitism — prejudice and discrimination against Jewish people. Over the past century it grew from a civil rights advocacy group into one of the most institutionally embedded private organisations in the United States political and media landscape. It is not elected, not regulated as an intelligence body, and answers to no government authority. Yet its influence over what speech is permitted online, what counts as hate, and how governments measure ethnic persecution now spans four continents.

The ADL supplied the definition of antisemitism used by the United States government — specifically the International Holocaust Remembrance Alliance (IHRA) working definition, which it helped draft and which has since been adopted by over 40 national governments, hundreds of universities, and numerous corporate compliance frameworks. It advises YouTube and Meta (Facebook's parent company) on content moderation standards. Its financial affiliate, JLens, manages $12 billion in communal investment capital from 35 Jewish institutions. It operates a legal network across more than 50 major law firms. It runs a 103-country antisemitism surveillance platform. And it coordinates — at conference level — with the South African Jewish Board of Deputies (SAJBD), which briefs the United States government's National Security Council on South African affairs.

None of this institutional reach derives from an elected mandate. It derives from one thing: donor money, and the claimed authority to define what hate is. Those two things are not separable. The ADL raised $91 million in 2020 alone. Its revenue surges when its donors perceive rising threat. Its donors are US-based, liberal-leaning, Jewish professionals. They have no stake in what happens to Afrikaners in South Africa. And so the ADL produces nothing — not because the evidence is absent, but because there is no revenue in recognising it.

The Standard Applied

The ADL's Own Six Criteria — Each One Applied to Afrikaners

Who are Afrikaners?

Afrikaners are an ethnic group of approximately 2.7 million people, predominantly descended from Dutch, German, and French Huguenot settlers who arrived in South Africa from 1652 onwards. Their language, Afrikaans, is a distinct language derived from Dutch. The majority are Christian (Reformed Protestant tradition) and many are farmers whose families have worked the same land for generations. Under the National Party government (1948–1994), Afrikaners were the dominant political constituency — a historical fact now routinely deployed to dismiss contemporary Afrikaner concerns about discrimination and violence as expressions of racial nostalgia rather than genuine ethnic vulnerability. That conflation ignores that the NP government was countering an internationally backed armed insurgency that used civilian-targeted bombings and township violence throughout this period. The ADL's own published article applies the conflation directly. The ADL's own published article does exactly this.

The ADL publishes its methodology for identifying ethnic targeting that requires institutional response. These six criteria are drawn from the ADL's own published standards — the same standards it applies globally when identifying hate speech, incitement, and ethnic persecution. Each is applied to the documented Afrikaner case below.
