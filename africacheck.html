Africa Check: The Gatekeepers of African Truth

Africa Check is the continent's most cited fact-checking organisation. It is housed at the University of the Witwatersrand (Wits), a public research university in Johannesburg. It determines what Meta (the parent company of Facebook and Instagram) labels as "false information" across sub-Saharan Africa. Its methodology is shaped by a single academic at Stellenbosch University. Its funding comes from a handful of international foundations. Its founder now sits at the body that certifies who qualifies as a fact-checker worldwide. This page maps the funding, institutional connections, editorial pipeline, and structural power behind the organisation that decides what three billion people are allowed to see.

Organisation Profile

What is Africa Check?

Africa Check is a non-profit trust registered in South Africa (IT000728/2015(C)) and as a foundation in Kenya. It was founded in 2012 by British journalist Peter Cunliffe-Jones at the African Investigative Journalism Conference in Johannesburg. It is physically housed at the University of the Witwatersrand (Wits), a public research university in Johannesburg that has received at least $3.9 million from the Open Society Foundations (OSF, the international grantmaking network founded by Hungarian-American financier George Soros) across 16 documented grants. Africa Check operates offices in Nigeria and Senegal and coordinates the Africa Facts Network, which has grown from 2 organisations in 2017 to over 59 member organisations across the continent by October 2025.

Africa Check is accredited by the International Fact-Checking Network (IFCN, a programme of the Poynter Institute, a non-profit journalism school in Florida that owns PolitiFact and sets global fact-checking standards). This accreditation gave Africa Check authority within Meta's (the parent company of Facebook and Instagram) third-party fact-checking programme. Content flagged by Africa Check as "false" was downranked or labelled across Facebook and Instagram for billions of users globally. Africa Check is cited as an authority by CNN, PBS, Snopes, the BBC, Al Jazeera, and Wikipedia.

No Afrikaans-language media outlet has any equivalent international connection to any fact-checking body, any international media partner, or any technology platform's content moderation programme. This structural isolation is a documented finding.

Example #1

The Chinese Police Stations Fact-Check

Rating: "False." Status: Never retracted or updated.

Africa Check rated claims about Chinese police stations in South Africa as false in November 2018.

The fact-check relied on two sources: a denial from a South African Police Service (SAPS) spokesperson (who said the facility was for Mandarin lessons) and a denial from the Chinese Embassy in South Africa (which called the reports "totally fake news"). Africa Check accepted both denials as dispositive evidence. News24 (a major South African news website owned by Media24/Naspers) republished the rating in May 2020.

In September 2022, the Madrid-based human rights NGO Safeguard Defenders documented 102 Chinese overseas police service stations in 53 countries, linked to the Chinese Communist Party's (CCP) United Front Work Department. South Africa was specifically named with 13 centres. In December 2022, CNN published an exclusive confirming the global network. In April 2023, the FBI arrested two men in New York for operating a secret police station on behalf of the Fuzhou branch of China's Ministry of Public Security. Investigations were launched in at least 12 countries. The Africa-China Reporting Project (also based at Wits, where Africa Check is housed) published a nuanced analysis confirming the South African centres' connection to the CCP's overseas apparatus.

As of March 2026, Africa Check's 2018 fact-check has not been retracted, updated, or annotated with a note acknowledging the subsequent findings.

Example #3

The Revolving Door Between Fact-Checker and Certification Body

Pattern: Personnel rotation between stages of the pipeline

Africa Check's founder moved to the body that certifies fact-checkers, where he wrote the rules that certify his own former organisation.

Peter Cunliffe-Jones founded Africa Check at Wits in 2012. He left in May 2019 to become Senior Adviser at the IFCN (International Fact-Checking Network) at the Poynter Institute. He had previously co-written the IFCN Code of Principles in 2016, the standard that determines which organisations Meta uses for content moderation. He revised the code in 2019-2020 after joining the IFCN. He was subsequently named to TikTok's Sub-Saharan Africa Safety Advisory Council and co-directs the Chevening African Media Freedom Fellowship (funded by the UK Foreign Office).

The individual who built the fact-checker wrote the rules that certify fact-checkers, advises the social media platforms that enforce those rules, and trains the next generation of journalists who apply them. This is documented institutional positioning, not an allegation of wrongdoing.
