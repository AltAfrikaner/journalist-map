Before You Read

What This Investigation Is About

In the early 1990s, the white South African government and the African National Congress (ANC) negotiated the end of apartheid. The deal included a promise: nobody would be prosecuted for the system of apartheid itself. Individual acts of violence could be investigated through a Truth and Reconciliation Commission (TRC), but the political system would not be treated as a criminal offence. That was the price of a peaceful transition.

This investigation documents, using only public records, how that deal was systematically reversed after 1994 by the same ANC government that agreed to it, and how the legal instrument created in that reversal was then deployed internationally against Israel and, potentially, against the broader Western legal order.

Every claim on this page is sourced to official United Nations (UN) records, court judgments, academic publications, or verified news reporting. Nothing is fabricated. Nothing is speculative. The documentary record speaks for itself.

Key Terms Used on This Page

ANC = African National Congress. The liberation movement that fought apartheid and has governed South Africa since 1994.

NP = National Party. The white-minority government that created and administered apartheid from 1948 to 1994.

TRC = Truth and Reconciliation Commission. The body created in 1995 to investigate apartheid-era human rights violations and grant amnesty to those who confessed.

ICC = International Criminal Court. A permanent court in The Hague, Netherlands, created by the Rome Statute in 1998 to prosecute genocide, war crimes, and crimes against humanity.

ICJ = International Court of Justice. The UN's main judicial body, also in The Hague. Settles disputes between countries. This is where South Africa filed its genocide case against Israel in December 2023.

Rome Statute = The international treaty that created the ICC. Adopted on 17 July 1998 at a diplomatic conference in Rome, Italy. Entered into force on 1 July 2002.

SADC = Southern African Development Community. A regional bloc of 16 southern African countries. South Africa is its dominant member.

PGA = Parliamentarians for Global Action. An international network of legislators that worked with the ANC government on the Rome Statute.

Customary international law = Unwritten rules of international law that arise from consistent state practice and acceptance. Section 232 of South Africa's Constitution says customary international law is automatically law in South Africa.

Strategic Framework

Three Levels of Deployment

The universalisation of apartheid as a crime against humanity was not a single act. It was a three-level strategic operation that targeted different adversaries at different timescales, using the same legal instrument.

What "Three Levels" Means

When a government creates a new legal tool, the question is always: who is it aimed at? In this case, the evidence shows the universalised crime of apartheid was designed to operate at three levels simultaneously. Level 1 keeps the Afrikaner community permanently under legal threat inside South Africa. Level 2 deploys the same tool against Israel on the world stage. Level 3 establishes a precedent that any Western-aligned country can be targeted using the same framework. Each level uses the same legal instrument (Article 7(1)(j) of the Rome Statute), but applies it to a different target at a different scale.

Think of it as one weapon with three barrels, each pointed in a different direction, fired at different times.

Level 1 — Immediate Target

Collective Afrikaner Guilt

By walking back the negotiated amnesty domestically (1995 TRC Act) and codifying apartheid as a universal crime internationally (1998 Rome Statute), the ANC government created a permanent moral lever against the Afrikaner community. This lever does not need to be pulled often. Its value is in its existence. It says: at any time, for any reason, your past can be prosecuted. This is what the COSAS Four case (2021/2025) demonstrates. The 14-year suppression of prosecutions (2003 to 2017) shows the ANC government could turn prosecution on or off depending on political need.

Level 2 — Medium-Term Target

The State of Israel

The universalisation of the apartheid definition (removing "Southern Africa" from the legal text) in 1998, the Durban I deployment in 2001, and the ICJ case in 2023 form a continuous arc. Iran's role from the Tehran regional meeting (February 2001) onwards was to chair and coordinate the deployment of the "Israel equals apartheid" template. Iran has geopolitical reasons to target Israel that predate this: the 1979 Revolution positioned Iran as the primary state opponent of Zionism. The ANC-Iran Joint Commission (1995) and the nuclear discussions (1997) were the institutional glue.

Level 3 — Strategic Target

The Western Liberal Order

By universalising "apartheid" as a crime against humanity and demonstrating it could be deployed against a Western-aligned state (Israel), the ANC-Iran-SADC-Global South bloc created a legal weapon that could, in theory, be used against any state that practices what the bloc defines as "systematic oppression and domination by one racial group." The US and UK voted against the 1973 Convention for precisely this reason: they understood it was being designed as a portable political weapon, not a fixed legal instrument. The Rome Statute proved them right.

Iran's Strategic Exemption

STATUS: Signed the Rome Statute. Never ratified.

Iran positioned itself outside the ICC's jurisdiction while pushing for apartheid to be a universal crime aimed at Israel. Iran built the weapon while exempting itself from the armoury. Egypt, Russia, Sudan, Syria, and the United States also signed but never ratified. Israel never signed.

Why This Matters

The 1993 Interim Constitution used the word "shall" when it said amnesty would be granted. In legal language, "shall" means "must." It is not optional. The NP (white government) understood this to mean all politically motivated acts would be pardoned. This was their price for handing over power peacefully.

But the 1995 TRC Act, drafted by ANC Justice Minister Dullah Omar, added new conditions: applicants had to make "full disclosure of all relevant facts," prove their acts were politically motivated, and face public hearings. Many applicants were denied amnesty under these stricter rules. The FW de Klerk Foundation later described this as the Act having "circumvented the clear intention of the interim Constitution."

Why the Removal of "Southern Africa" Changes Everything

The 1973 Convention on Apartheid was written specifically about South Africa and its neighbours. It named "Southern Africa" in its text. This meant it could only be used against South Africa, Namibia, and the Portuguese colonies. It was a targeted weapon.

The 1998 Rome Statute definition removed every mention of "Southern Africa." The crime of apartheid became portable, meaning it could be applied to any country, anywhere, at any time, if someone could argue that an "institutionalised regime of systematic oppression and domination by one racial group over any other" existed there.

This is why the definition was later applied to Israel. The universalisation was not accidental. It was the entire point of the late insertion.

What Happened at Durban

The United Nations organised a World Conference Against Racism in Durban, South Africa, in September 2001 (just days before the September 11 attacks in the United States). It was supposed to fight racism worldwide. Instead, it became the launchpad for the global campaign to label Israel an "apartheid state."

A parallel NGO Forum, attended by 8,000 representatives from 3,000 organisations, adopted a declaration calling Israel a "racist, apartheid state" guilty of "racist crimes including war crimes, acts of genocide and ethnic cleansing." Copies of the antisemitic forgery The Protocols of the Elders of Zion were sold at the conference venue. The United States and Israel walked out.

The Ford Foundation provided USD 10 million in funding. The Rockefeller Foundation, MacArthur Foundation, and Mott Foundation also contributed. The conference was chaired by South Africa's Foreign Minister. The template document had been prepared at the Tehran preparatory meeting chaired by Iran five months earlier. Fourteen Western democracies later boycotted Durban follow-up conferences.

Critics described the outcome as the "Durban Strategy," a deliberate plan to delegitimise Israel by applying the universalised apartheid definition that had been inserted into the Rome Statute three years earlier.

The Precedent Case

COSAS Four: What It Is Really For

On 14 April 2025, the Johannesburg High Court confirmed charges of murder and apartheid as crimes against humanity against two former apartheid-era security police officers for a 1982 bombing that killed three student activists. Judge Dario Dosio ruled that customary international law applied directly via Section 232 of the Constitution, that apartheid had been a crime against humanity at the time, and that there was no statute of limitations.

This was not primarily about two elderly defendants. It was a precedent case that established three legal principles simultaneously:

Principle Established

1. Apartheid was a crime under customary international law before 1994

This bypasses the Rome Statute's non-retroactivity bar entirely.

Principle Established

2. No statute of limitations for crimes against humanity

Apartheid-era acts can be prosecuted indefinitely, regardless of when they occurred.

Principle Established

3. Customary international law applies directly in SA courts

Via Section 232 of the Constitution. No new legislation required.

Once these principles are established domestically, they become available for deployment:

The ICJ case against Israel (South Africa can point to its own courts prosecuting apartheid). Future ICC prosecution of Israeli officials using the universalised apartheid definition. And the question the Southern Africa Litigation Centre asked in April 2025: "What about banks, arms dealers and multinational corporations that profited and supported the apartheid regime?"

"The tremors caused by the judgment from courtroom 4D will affect many other cases that have been filed, and others that are yet to be filed."Southern Africa Litigation Centre, April 2025

The ANC government suppressed apartheid-era prosecutions for 14 years (2003-2017). The Supreme Court of Appeal called this "perplexing and inexplicable." Then the prosecutions resumed in 2021, two years before the ICJ case was filed in December 2023. The domestic precedent was needed when the Israel case was maturing, not before.

The Core Finding

The Deal They Broke

The Groote Schuur Minute (4 May 1990) established the framework: a working group on "the granting of immunity in respect of political offences" that would "bear in mind experiences in Namibia." The 1993 Interim Constitution's post-amble mandated that amnesty "shall be granted."

De Klerk's consistent position from his 1997 TRC submission to his 2020 statement was that the 1973 Convention had been "little more than a mobilisation exercise by the African National Congress and its totalitarian and Third World supporters in the UN General Assembly" and that it was "never adopted or approved by the Security Council."

On the narrow legal-historical facts, he was correct. The 1973 Convention was Soviet-initiated (drafts submitted by the USSR and Guinea in 1971). It was never Security Council binding. No Western democracy ratified it. And it was abandoned by the UN General Assembly in 1990, the year negotiations began.

What De Klerk did not anticipate was that his own Justice Minister (Omar) would simultaneously tighten domestic amnesty conditions and insert a universalised apartheid crime into the Rome Statute through a late SADC proposal at Rome, using a definition stripped of all references to Southern Africa, making it a portable legal weapon deployable against any state the Global South chose to target.

The FW de Klerk Foundation itself acknowledged: "One of the greatest failures of the NP's negotiators was their inability to conclude a comprehensive amnesty agreement before the 1994 elections."
