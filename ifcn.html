Section 01 — Executive Summary

The System Nobody Voted For

The International Fact-Checking Network (IFCN), housed at the Poynter Institute in Florida, is the singular global certification body that determines which organisations are eligible to moderate content on Meta (Facebook, Instagram, Threads) platforms serving approximately three billion users. Its Code of Principles, assessed by self-selected assessors and governed by a board composed of the organisations it certifies, constitutes the most consequential chokepoint in global information flow that most citizens have never examined.

Key Finding

The Parent Company's President Sits on the Board

Neil Brown, President of the Poynter Institute (which owns the IFCN), sits on the IFCN Advisory Board as an "independent member." He also founded PolitiFact, which is itself an IFCN signatory. The man who runs the parent organisation, and who created one of the certified organisations, participates in decisions governing certification of all other organisations.

⚠ Structural conflict: Independent members reportedly "do not cast votes for incoming applications" but "actively participate in all other decisions," including suspension, withdrawal, complaints adjudication, Code revisions, and grant allocation.
Key Finding

The Annual Transparency Report Has Stopped

The IFCN's Complaints Policy promises an annual report on complaints received and responses. This commitment appears unfulfilled since 2020. The mechanism designed to provide public accountability has simply stopped functioning. The complaints process is email-only with no tracking, no acknowledgment requirement, and no response timeline.

⚠ Accountability gap: The IFCN simultaneously built a "tighter complaints system" to filter "frivolous submissions," which may also filter out legitimate complaints from the public.
Key Finding

Claim Selection Is Invisible

The Code of Principles addresses nonpartisanship in claim verification but provides zero mechanism to audit nonpartisanship in claim selection. Which claims get chosen for fact-checking is an editorial decision with enormous power to shape discourse. No signatory is required to publish data on the political or ideological distribution of their fact-checks.

⚠ Unauditable power: A fact-checker that checks 90% conservative claims and 10% progressive claims could theoretically comply with Principle 1 if each individual check meets the same evidential standard. The most powerful editorial decision in the system is structurally invisible.
Section 02 — Mechanism-of-Action

The Closed Funding Loop

The IFCN operates within a self-certifying circular system. Follow the arrows. You arrive back where you started. No external audit exists at any node.

How the loop works

Self-Certifying, Self-Funding, Self-Protecting

Google and YouTube fund the IFCN ($13.2M Global Fact Check Fund). The IFCN certifies fact-checkers (182 across 57 countries). Meta (Facebook, Instagram, Threads) requires IFCN certification for its Third-Party Fact-Checking Programme. Meta pays the certified fact-checkers for articles linked to flagged posts. The fact-checkers produce ratings. The ratings suppress content (80%+ distribution reduction for "False" ratings). The suppression is cited as evidence of public service. The public service justifies continued funding. The funding sustains the certification. The loop closes.

⚠ USAID confirmed: The IFCN's own director, Angie Drobnic Holan, confirmed in a January 2026 Poynter article that the United States Agency for International Development (USAID) had been funding fact-checkers in "Eastern Europe, Africa, Latin America and Asia." When Trump's administration ended USAID, Holan publicly mourned the loss of that funding stream. The US government was directly funding the same fact-checking ecosystem that suppresses content on Facebook globally.
⚠ Wasserman Pipeline: Professor Herman Wasserman (Stellenbosch University) defines "disinformation" for Africa, edits African Journalism Studies, and is commissioned by Africa Check (funded by the Open Society Foundations (OSF) 12%, Luminate 8%, Gates Foundation 13%). His framework feeds through Africa Check into the IFCN via co-founder Peter Cunliffe-Jones, then into Meta content moderation, resulting in the suppression of Afrikaner farm attack posts. One professor at one South African university feeds a framework that reaches three billion users.
⚠ TikTok / ByteDance: TikTok (owned by ByteDance) is listed as an IFCN platform partner. Poynter confirmed receiving payment from ByteDance for fact-checking and media literacy work but declined to disclose the amount. ByteDance's internal corporate documents, leaked in 2020, showed at least 60 of 138 managers were members of an internal Communist Party of China (CPC) oversight committee. No direct CPC-to-IFCN funding has been documented, but the platform partnership means CPC-linked commercial revenue flows into the Poynter ecosystem.
Section 03 — Advisory Board

Structural Analysis

Geographic Gaps in "Global" Governance

No board members from the United Kingdom, France, Germany, Canada, Australia, Scandinavia, Eastern Europe (except Bosnia), Central Asia, or the Pacific. The world's oldest established democracies with the longest traditions of press freedom have zero representation on the body that governs global fact-checking certification.

⚠ Guild dynamic: Certified organisations vote on whether their peers, competitors, and potential partners receive certification. Dapo Olorunyomi (Premium Times/Dubawa) sits on BOTH the IFCN Advisory Board AND the Africa Check advisory board. The same personnel circulate through the same governance structures at multiple levels.
Section 04 — Funding Architecture

Follow the Money

The IFCN's funding trail, assembled from the Poynter Institute's own disclosures and press releases. A remarkably concentrated ecosystem where the same funders appear at every level of the certification chain.

Technology

Google / YouTube — $13.2 Million

Global Fact Check Fund (2022-2025), administered by the IFCN. $8.5M+ distributed to 174 organisations in 72 countries by mid-2025. Three tiers: BUILD ($25K), GROW ($50K), ENGAGE ($100K). Google also funds MediaWise (Stanford partnership), individual fact-checker grants, and provides direct grants to Africa Check via the Google News Initiative.

Platform Revenue

Meta (Facebook / Instagram / Threads) — Commercial Payments

Pays certified fact-checkers per article linked to flagged posts. FactCheck.org disclosed receiving $162,800 from Meta in its 2024 fiscal year. For many organisations, especially in India and the Global South, Meta payments are the primary revenue source. The US programme was terminated in January 2025. The international programme remains active across approximately 115 countries.

Foundations

Open Society Foundations (OSF) — $492,000+ (2016-2020)

$300,000 disclosed in 2017 press release. $492,000 total between 2016-2020 per Media Research Center analysis. The OSF also funds Africa Check directly (12% of revenue per documented analysis) and multiple other IFCN signatories globally.

US Government

United States Agency for International Development (USAID)

IFCN Director Angie Drobnic Holan confirmed in a January 2026 Poynter article: "The abrupt ending of USAID meant an immediate end to funding independent international journalism, which included support for fact-checkers in Eastern Europe, Africa, Latin America and Asia." USAID also funded the Media Institute of Southern Africa (MISA) and the Alliance for Open Society International (AOSI), a separate entity set up specifically to receive US government funding. When Trump cut USAID, he severed the US government's direct funding line into the global fact-checking ecosystem.

Foundations

Omidyar Network / Luminate — $1,000,000+

$1M grant disclosed in 2017 Poynter press release. Luminate (an Omidyar entity) listed as ongoing Poynter funder. Pierre Omidyar (eBay founder) network also funds multiple fact-checking organisations globally.

Foundations

Bill & Melinda Gates Foundation — $383,000+

$383,000 earmarked through Poynter in 2015 for health fact-checking. Additional $126,650 to PolitiFact for Africa Check partnership. The Columbia Journalism Review (2020) found 16 examples of Africa Check examining claims related to Gates, raising questions about editorial independence when the funder is also the subject.

US Government

National Endowment for Democracy (NED) / State Department

The NED is listed as an IFCN funder in the 2017 Poynter press release. The NED receives $300M+ in annual congressional appropriation. The Biden-era State Department sub-granted $275,219 to Poynter via World Learning Inc. (2023-2024). Poynter received $737,400 in Paycheck Protection Programme (PPP) federal loans. Historical training contracts with Voice of America (US government-funded media).

Section 05 — Examples

Who Fact-Checks the Fact-Checkers?

Two case studies that reveal the structural failure of the IFCN fact-checking model. In both cases, the methodology produced ratings that served the interests of the party being investigated. The question is not whether individual fact-checks contain errors. The question is whether a methodology that treats official denials as dispositive evidence can ever produce reliable results.

Act I — Setup

The Viral Claim

21 March 2026. Right Angle News Network posts on X: Irish band Kneecap's concert in Cuba consumed massive electricity, causing a blackout that killed ventilator patients at a Havana hospital. 6.8 million views. Spreads to Facebook.

Act II — The Fact-Check

Ireland's Sole Gatekeeper

TheJournal.ie, Ireland's only IFCN-certified fact-checker (since 2017), Meta partner since 2018, European Union (EU)-funded through the European Digital Media Observatory (EDMO) Ireland hub, rates the claim FALSE. Hospital confirmed no ventilator deaths. Cuba's energy ministry attributed blackout to plant failure 540km away. The narrow verdict is correct.

Act III — The Irony

The Constellation

Cuba maintains an embassy in Dublin (since 2001) and in Pretoria. Ireland formally intervened in South Africa's ICJ genocide case against Israel in January 2025. The fact-checker, the band, the geopolitical alignment, and the diplomatic relationship all share the same axis. No competing Irish fact-checker exists within the system.

Act IV — The Methodology

Ask the Accused

The IFCN model requires fact-checkers to "seek where possible to contact those who made the claim." In practice, this means contacting the government being accused. Cuba denied the concert caused the blackout. Even if events had occurred as claimed, why would the Cuban government confirm it? The methodology treats official denial as evidence. The defendant's plea becomes the verdict.

Case Study 2 — Chinese Police Stations

When Africa Check Protected the Chinese Government's Denial

In November 2018, Africa Check published a fact-check titled "No, Chinese people aren't opening their own police stations in South Africa." They relied on a South African Police Service (SAPS) spokesperson who said the facility was "not a police station" and the Chinese Embassy which called the reports "totally fake news." Africa Check rated the claim FALSE.

In September 2022, the Madrid-based human rights non-governmental organisation (NGO) Safeguard Defenders documented 102 Chinese overseas police service stations in 53 countries. South Africa was named with 13 centres. In April 2023, the Federal Bureau of Investigation (FBI) arrested two men in New York for operating a secret Chinese police station on behalf of China's Ministry of Public Security. Fourteen countries launched investigations. Canada summoned China's ambassador. The Netherlands and Ireland demanded closures.

Africa Check has not retracted its 2018 fact-check. For four years, anyone sharing accurate information about Chinese police infrastructure in South Africa was potentially flagged as sharing "false information" on Facebook, based on Africa Check's debunk, which was based on the Chinese government's own denial.

⚠ The pattern: The methodology is identical in both cases. Contact the accused party. Receive their denial. Present the denial as the verdict. When the SAPS said "not a police station" and the Chinese Embassy said "fake news," Africa Check treated those statements as dispositive evidence. The same methodology is applied to farm attack statistics, political violence claims, and any narrative that an official source has an interest in denying.

The Central Question

Who Fact-Checks the Fact-Checkers?

The IFCN certifies fact-checkers. Meta enforces their ratings. Google funds their operations. But no external body audits whether the fact-checks themselves are accurate. When Africa Check rated Chinese police stations as "false," no mechanism corrected the error for four years. When the FBI made arrests confirming the claims Africa Check had debunked, no system restored the posts that had been suppressed. The fact-checker's error became permanent algorithmic truth. The question the entire system refuses to answer: who holds the fact-checkers accountable when they are wrong?

⚠ Structural answer: Nobody. The IFCN's Complaints Policy explicitly states it "does not intervene in specific complaints." Individual fact-check errors are not the IFCN's concern. The platforms that enforce the ratings do not audit them. The funders who pay for the system do not review its outputs. The annual transparency report that was supposed to provide public accountability has not been published since 2020. The system is structurally incapable of correcting itself.
Section 06 — Logic Chain

The Complaints Dead Letter

The IFCN's own website provides the evidence. Each step in this chain uses only documented statements from ifcncodeofprinciples.poynter.org.

IF
The IFCN promises an annual transparency report on complaints received and responses
AND
No such report has been published since 2020
THEN
The accountability mechanism is non-functional. The public has no visibility into how many complaints were filed, investigated, or dismissed.
IF
Complaints are email-only with no tracking system, no acknowledgment requirement, and no response timeline
THEN
Complainants have zero visibility into whether their complaint was received, reviewed, or dismissed.
IF
Every decision at every tier of the complaints ladder is made by the IFCN Director and Advisory Board
AND
The Advisory Board is composed of representatives from the certified organisations being complained about
THEN
The system being complained about is adjudicating the complaints about itself. There is no external ombudsman, no independent arbitration panel, and no right of appeal to any body outside the IFCN.
Bottom Line

A Membership Agreement for a Private Club

The IFCN Code of Principles creates the appearance of rigorous, independent, transparent accountability while containing structural features that ensure the IFCN itself remains accountable to no one. The system's five principles govern the certified but contain no equivalent principles governing the certifier. It is a guild: an industry body that sets its own standards, certifies its own members, disciplines its own ranks, and derives its power from an exclusive relationship with the platforms that enforce its ratings on billions of users.

"The Code of Principles is not a constitution. It is a membership agreement for a private club. And the private club controls what three billion people are allowed to read."
