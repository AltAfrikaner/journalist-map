The Indictment

A federal grand jury in Montgomery, Alabama returned an 11-count indictment on 21 April 2026. Acting Attorney General Todd Blanche announced the charges: wire fraud, false statements to a federally insured bank, and conspiracy to commit money laundering. The core allegation: between 2014 and 2023, the SPLC secretly funnelled more than $3 million in donor funds to paid informants inside the KKK, Aryan Nations, the National Socialist Movement, and the alleged organisers of the 2017 Charlottesville Unite the Right rally. The same groups the SPLC publicly claimed to be dismantling.

"The SPLC was not dismantling these groups. It was instead manufacturing the extremism it purports to oppose by paying sources to stoke racial hatred." Acting Attorney General Todd Blanche, DOJ press conference, 21 April 2026

To disguise the payments, the SPLC allegedly created fictitious shell entities including accounts labelled "Fox Photography" and "Rare Books Warehouse" to transfer donor money to extremist group members. Not one donor was ever told. The SPLC's own website simultaneously ran campaigns asking the public to fund the fight against the very organisations allegedly receiving those payments.

The Charlottesville Informant

One informant, designated F-37 in the DOJ indictment, was a member of the online leadership chat group that planned the 2017 Charlottesville rally. Per the indictment, he attended at SPLC's direction, made racist postings under SPLC supervision, and helped coordinate transportation for several attendees. He was allegedly paid $270,000 by the SPLC between 2015 and 2023. SPLC denies all allegations.

Charlottesville produced one death. SPLC citations in media spiked 6,600% that week. SPLC revenue jumped from $51M to $133M in twelve months. Apple gave $1 million. JPMorgan Chase gave $500,000. George and Amal Clooney gave $1 million. They were donating to an organisation that an indictment would later allege had helped organise the event that prompted their donations.

FBI and DOJ Coordination

A House Judiciary Committee investigation found 13 internal FBI documents citing SPLC data, including a Richmond Field Office memo designating traditional Latin Mass Catholics as potential domestic terrorism risks. One FBI agent noted internally that "our overreliance on the SPLC for hate designation is problematic." In October 2025, FBI Director Kash Patel severed all ties with the SPLC, calling it a "partisan smear machine." The Biden Department of Justice had given SPLC employees early access to federal law enforcement data and held regular co-ordination meetings.

The Editorial-Advertising Dual Relationship

MSNBC cited the SPLC 246 times between 2017 and 2026, representing 63% of all documented cable TV citations, and conducted 35 on-air SPLC interviews. From late 2024, MSNBC simultaneously ran a 120-second SPLC fundraising advertisement hundreds of times, generating millions in advertising revenue. Documenting Hate: The Soros-Funded Data Laundry

In 2017, ProPublica, funded by Soros's Foundation to Promote Open Society, launched the Documenting Hate project. SPLC supplied unverified incident reports. Google News Lab built a machine-learning index. More than 100 newsrooms, including the New York Times, the Guardian, NBC, BuzzFeed, and Vox, drew from the database and published the resulting stories under their own mastheads without disclosing the SPLC origin.

A Poynter freelancer "who typically works for SPLC" wrote content for the IFCN, the international fact-checking certification network that makes Africa Check Meta's official content moderator for South Africa. The SPLC-Poynter-IFCN-Africa Check-Meta chain is the route through which SPLC methodology reached South African content moderation decisions.

The Stolen Document Pipeline

The DOJ indictment documents that one SPLC informant broke into an extremist group's headquarters, stole 25 boxes of documents, and sent copies to an SPLC employee who knew they were stolen. A separate informant was paid $6,000 to falsely confess to the theft. Material from those stolen documents was later used in Hatewatch stories, which partner newsrooms subsequently reported as independent journalism.
